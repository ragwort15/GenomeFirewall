01The Problem

The lab is too slow. The bug isn't.

Antibiotic susceptibility testing takes one to three days. During that window, clinicians treat blind — best-guess broad-spectrum coverage against a bacterium whose resistance profile they cannot yet see.

Every ineffective dose costs the patient time and gives resistant strains another chance to outrun the drug. The system that keeps modern medicine possible — routine surgery, transplants, chemotherapy — depends on antibiotics still working. That guarantee is failing.

Resistance is written into the genome long before the culture plate reveals it.

02The Solution

Read the genome. Return an audited call.

Once a bacterial isolate has been sequenced and assembled, Genome Firewall reads the reconstructed FASTA and produces, for each antibiotic on its panel: likely to work, likely to fail, or an explicit no-call.

Every result carries a calibrated confidence score and an evidence category — a known resistance gene, a statistical association, or no signal found. The report is decision support, not a treatment decision, and it says so on every page.

Assembled FASTA → feature matrix. AMRFinderPlus + Kleborate detect known AMR genes and resistance-associated mutations.

03The Safety Layer

Two rules the model isn't allowed to break.

The classifier decides — but it decides under supervision. Two deterministic rules sit above the probability and can veto it. Both exist because the failure modes they prevent are the ones a clinician cannot afford.

·
The drug's molecular target
Every antibiotic works by binding to a specific bacterial component — the target. Ciprofloxacin binds DNA gyrase. Vancomycin binds a peptide terminus in the cell wall. If the target isn't encoded in the genome, the drug has nothing to attack, regardless of what resistance genes are or aren't present.
I
Target-presence gate
Before the probability is trusted, the pipeline confirms the drug's target is present in the assembly. Missing target → the verdict is forced to likely to fail. This blocks a specific false-positive: the model returning “likely to work” simply because it saw no resistance markers, when in truth the drug has nothing to bind.
II
No-call threshold
Predictions whose calibrated probability sits inside a band of low confidence — near a coin flip, or in a region where the model's calibration curve is unreliable — are returned as no-call instead of a verdict. The threshold is fit on a held-out calibration set, not chosen by feel. Abstaining is a feature, not a failure: a shaky answer in a treatment decision is worse than an honest “wait for the lab.”

The gate stops the model from being fooled. The threshold stops the model from pretending to know.

04The Models

From FASTA to a decision, in one agent loop.

05What's New

Where we go beyond the baseline.

A regularized logistic regression on gene presence-absence is the reference baseline. We keep it as a control — and add four things a bare classifier doesn't give a clinician.

01 · Target-presence gate

The model can't fake a “works.”

If the drug's molecular target is absent from the genome, the deterministic gate forces likely to fail — a class of false-positive the classifier alone can't rule out.

02 · Honest no-call

Refuse when the evidence is thin.

Predictions below the calibration-set threshold return no-call instead of a coin-flip verdict. The clinician learns exactly when to wait for the culture plate.

03 · Evidence categories

Separate mechanism from correlation.

Each call is tagged as either a known resistance gene, a statistical association, or no signal — so a SHAP score is never mistaken for a biological cause.

04 · Clinician-first UI

A report, not a probability dump.

Patient context, per-drug cards with calibrated confidence bars, and a persistent lab-confirmation banner — designed for the moment of a treatment decision, not a model dashboard.

06The Hard Parts

What we're fighting.

The challenge isn't training a classifier. It's making one that a clinician should be allowed to see.

  • Data leakage
    Near-identical genomes across train and test inflate every score. We split by sequence homology and report metrics only on held-out groups the model has never seen.
  • Class imbalance
    Some drugs have far more resistant than susceptible isolates (and vice versa). Balanced accuracy, per-class recall, and PR-AUC per drug — not a single headline number — are what we report.
  • Calibration
    A 0.9 probability must mean roughly 90% correct across the dataset. We check with Brier scores and reliability plots, and tune the no-call threshold on a held-out calibration set.
  • Honest attribution
    A high SHAP value doesn't prove causation. We split “known mechanism detected” from “model saw a statistical pattern” so the evidence never overstates itself.
  • Dual-use guardrails
    The system only predicts and explains resistance that already exists. It cannot design, modify, or optimize an organism — by construction, not by policy.
